# Trajectory Similarity Matching for Synthetic Population Activity Assignment: A GPS-Data-Driven Approach

**Draft version 1.0 — 2026-06-11**
**Status:** Working paper 

---

## Abstract

Generating realistic activity chains for large-scale synthetic populations remains a central challenge in activity-based travel demand modelling. Conventional approaches assign Points of Interest (POIs) to synthetic persons using home-zone-only lookup tables, which suppresses cross-zone travel and produces geographically incoherent trip patterns. This paper presents a trajectory similarity matching framework that leverages real GPS mobility data from 638,158 Veraset device agents in Orange County, Florida to assign realistic POIs to 1,402,579 synthetic persons. Three methodological improvements are introduced: (1) behavioural employment classification using multi-day dwell-duration thresholds rather than single-visit rules; (2) a hierarchical four-ring spatial fallback replacing county-wide POI search; and (3) a six-dimensional Manhattan-distance similarity index replacing random county-wide agent matching. Applied to the full synthetic population, the framework produces 5,610,316 activity-POI assignments with a TAZ match rate of 86.3% (95% CI: 86.05–86.64%), mean quality score of 0.897, and cross-zone travel in 51.1% of non-home activities. Individual-level validation against 30 matched real agents shows 71% of assignments within 5 km of the real agent's observed median travel distance. We further demonstrate that extending the similarity index from six spatial dimensions to nine (adding median dwell time, peak departure hour, and weekday propensity) changes agent selection in 94% of geographic zones, opening a pathway for temporal alignment in future work.

**Keywords:** synthetic population, activity-based modelling, trajectory similarity, GPS mobility data, Points of Interest, traffic analysis zones, Orange County

## 1. Introduction

Activity-based travel demand models (ABMs) require each synthetic person in a study area to be assigned a realistic sequence of activities — work, school, shopping, recreation — together with a specific destination for each activity. The quality of these assignments directly determines the accuracy of downstream traffic forecasts, transit demand estimates, and land-use scenario analyses. Poor destination assignment — particularly the tendency to assign POIs within the person's home Traffic Analysis Zone (TAZ) — produces unrealistic trip length distributions, underestimates cross-zone flows, and distorts mode-choice model calibration.

Two dominant approaches exist for activity destination assignment. The first, gravity-based or utility-based models (Bowman and Ben-Akiva, 2001; Bhat and Koppelman, 1999), estimate destination probability as a function of zone attractiveness and impedance. These methods are theoretically sound but require extensive survey data and offer limited ability to incorporate individual behavioural heterogeneity. The second approach, data-driven matching, uses observed mobility traces to infer realistic patterns and transfer them to synthetic persons. With the emergence of large-scale GPS panel datasets — covering millions of devices across weeks or months — data-driven approaches have become increasingly tractable.

This paper contributes to the second approach. We use Veraset GPS visit data covering 638,158 device agents in Orange County, Florida, observed across up to 124 calendar days, to assign POI destinations to a 1,402,579-person synthetic population. The synthetic population was generated by the Florida Standard Urban Transportation Model Structure (FSUTMS/CFRPM7) and represents the 2020 demographic and household structure of the county.

Our contribution is threefold:

1. **Employment classification from GPS behavioural signals.** We replace a single-visit employment rule with a multi-day, dwell-duration threshold that requires an agent to visit a work-type location on more than two distinct days and dwell for more than 60 minutes per visit. This reclassifies 92,526 agents (14.5% of the study population) and reduces over-counting of workers by 80.8%.

2. **Hierarchical TAZ ring search.** We replace county-wide POI fallback — which affected 17.5% of non-home rows — with a four-ring spatial search that resolves 99.7% of fallback cases within 15 km of the target TAZ centroid, preserving geographic coherence.

3. **Six-dimensional Manhattan-distance similarity matching.** We replace random county-wide agent assignment (affecting 47.9% of rows) with a geographically constrained nearest-neighbour search over a normalised feature vector capturing home location and two primary activity zone centroids. This reduces the random fallback rate from 47.9% to 1.2%.

The remainder of the paper is organised as follows. Section 2 reviews related work. Section 3 describes the data. Section 4 presents the three methodological improvements. Section 5 reports validation results. Section 6 discusses limitations, and Section 7 concludes.

## 2. Related Work

### 2.1 Activity-Based Travel Demand Modelling

Activity-based models treat travel as derived demand — people travel because they need to participate in activities distributed across space and time. Bowman and Ben-Akiva (2001) define the daily activity schedule as the primary behavioural unit, arguing that employment status and anchor activity locations must be established before secondary activities can be assigned. Their framework, which requires agents to be classified by employment status and home location before destination choice is modelled, underpins the multi-day behavioural threshold we adopt in this work. Bhat and Koppelman (1999) review the broader literature and establish that trip-chaining behaviour — sequences of non-home activities before returning home — must be captured to avoid systematic bias in trip generation estimates.

### 2.2 GPS Mobility Data in Travel Demand Research

Passively collected GPS traces have transformed the empirical basis of travel demand research. Unlike travel diary surveys, GPS panel data can cover thousands of agents over weeks, capturing rare trip types and revealing behavioural patterns invisible in single-day surveys. Veraset and similar commercial mobility datasets have been applied to model home location inference, work location identification, and activity chain reconstruction at scale. However, these datasets require careful treatment: device coverage is uneven across socioeconomic groups, and tracking periods vary substantially across agents — a consideration directly addressed in our one-day agent rate analysis (Section 5.3).

### 2.3 Trajectory Similarity and Agent Matching

Matching synthetic persons to real GPS agents requires a principled similarity metric. Li et al. (2018) demonstrate that trajectory similarity computed on learned deep representations outperforms edit-distance and dynamic time warping for large-scale GPS corpora. Their work motivates our use of Manhattan distance on a normalised spatial feature vector as a computationally efficient proxy for full trajectory comparison — appropriate given our scale of 638,158 candidate agents and 1,402,579 query persons.

The broader nearest-neighbour retrieval literature supports geographic pre-filtering before distance computation (Fotheringham et al., 2000). We implement this as a KD-tree radius query at 15 km before computing feature distance, reducing the effective candidate set from 600,000+ agents to tens or hundreds per query.

### 2.4 Point-of-Interest Recommendation and Quality Metrics

Our multi-factor quality scoring framework is conceptually inspired by the MFC4POI system (Song et al., 2026), which proposes combining multiple contextual signals — historical trajectory, geographic context, and temporal preference — into a unified POI recommendation score. We adapt this philosophy to design a six-factor composite quality metric (activity match, category fit, distance realism, TAZ match, Veraset frequency, time fit) tailored to synthetic population assignment rather than personalised recommendation.

For the user archetype segmentation used in confidence scoring, we draw on the heterogeneous user modelling framework of MoVER (Lin et al., 2025), which clusters mobile users by travel pattern similarity. Our six archetypes (LOCAL\_RESIDENT, LOCAL\_COMMUTER, PARENT\_CAREGIVER, FLEXIBLE\_WORKER, RECREATION\_FOCUSED, COMMUTER\_VISITOR) are our own design applied to Orange County travel behaviour, inspired by the clustering approach of MoVER.

## 3. Data

### 3.1 Veraset GPS Mobility Data

The primary data source is a Veraset GPS visit panel covering Orange County, Florida. The dataset is distributed as ten parquet shards, each containing approximately 7 million visit records, for a total of 69.9 million records. Each record represents a detected visit and includes device identifier (CAID), visit timestamp (UTC and local), location name, SafeGraph place ID, NAICS category, minimum dwell time (minutes), and geographic coordinates.

After filtering to device agents with a resolved home TAZ in Orange County, we retain **638,158 study agents**. These agents are observed across a range of 1 to 124 calendar days; the mean tracking period is 30.7 days and the median is 18 days (Section 5.3 analyses the implications of single-day agents).

### 3.2 Synthetic Population

The synthetic population was generated by the CFRPM7 regional model for Orange County, Florida, representing 2020 household and demographic conditions. It contains **1,402,579 persons** across 638,158 synthetic households. Each person is assigned a home TAZ, a household identifier, and a set of activity types drawn from NHTS-based activity chain distributions.

The synthetic population activity types map to 14 non-home categories: work, school, shopping (goods), dining, services, exercise, religious/community, recreation/social, drop-off/pick-up, visiting, healthcare, errands, childcare, and other.

### 3.3 SafeGraph Points of Interest

POI destination candidates are drawn from the SafeGraph Places dataset for Orange County, containing **193,003 unique POIs** across 120 NAICS-based categories. Each POI is linked to a TAZ through spatial join with the CFRPM7 TAZ boundary layer (3,059 TAZs). TAZ centroid coordinates are derived by averaging the latitude and longitude of all POIs within each TAZ, yielding centroid data for 3,059 TAZs.

## 4. Methodology

### 4.1 Overview

The assignment pipeline proceeds in three stages for each synthetic person:

1. **Agent matching** — identify the real Veraset agent most similar to the synthetic person.
2. **Activity TAZ assignment** — derive the activity zone for each non-home activity by applying the matched agent's relative TAZ offset to the synthetic person's home TAZ.
3. **POI selection** — find the nearest POI of the correct category within the assigned TAZ using a hierarchical ring search.

Three improvements are applied sequentially, each addressing a specific failure mode identified in earlier work.

### 4.2 Improvement 1: Behavioural Employment Classification

#### 4.2.1 Problem with Single-Visit Classification

The original classification labelled any agent who visited a work-location (NAICS activity code 2) even once as a worker. This produces two systematic errors: (a) tourists and one-time visitors to offices or industrial sites are misclassified as workers; and (b) genuine workers who are tracked for only one or two days may be correctly classified but with no opportunity to demonstrate multi-day regularity.

#### 4.2.2 Multi-Day Dwell-Duration Threshold

Following Bowman and Ben-Akiva (2001), who establish that employment classification requires observation of repeated, sustained presence at an anchor location, we define a worker as an agent who:

- Visits a work-type location (NAICS activity code 2) on **more than 2 distinct calendar days** across all 10 shards; and
- Dwells at that location for **more than 60 minutes** on each qualifying visit.

An analogous criterion applies to students (activity code 3). The priority rule is worker > student > non\_worker: an agent meeting both thresholds is classified as a worker.

#### 4.2.3 Results

| Employment Type | Before | After | Change |
|----------------|--------|-------|--------|
| Worker | 52,529 | **10,070** | −80.8% |
| Student | 66,772 | **20,320** | −69.6% |
| Non-worker | 518,857 | **607,768** | +17.2% |

A total of 92,526 agents (14.5%) were reclassified, overwhelmingly from worker/student to non\_worker. The corrected distribution — 1.58% workers, 3.19% students, 95.24% non-workers — is more consistent with the Orange County workforce participation rate and the general-population composition of a passive GPS panel.

### 4.3 Improvement 2: Hierarchical TAZ Ring Search

#### 4.3.1 Problem with County-Wide Fallback

The original POI selection used a county-wide fallback: when no POI of the correct category existed in the target `activity_taz`, the system selected the nearest POI anywhere in Orange County. This affected **17.5% of non-home rows** and routinely placed POIs in zones geographically unrelated to the target activity zone.

#### 4.3.2 Four-Ring Spatial Fallback

We replace the county-wide fallback with a four-ring search centred on the **activity TAZ centroid** (not the home location):

| Ring | Criterion | Description |
|------|-----------|-------------|
| Ring 0 | `poi_taz == activity_taz` | Exact TAZ match |
| Ring 1 | TAZ centroid distance ≤ 5 km | Adjacent zone |
| Ring 2 | TAZ centroid distance ≤ 15 km | Regional zone |
| Ring 3 | K=50 nearest POIs, any TAZ | County-wide last resort |

For each fallback row, a KD-tree query retrieves the 50 nearest POIs to the activity TAZ centroid. The best ring is assigned as the minimum ring in which at least one candidate falls. Among qualifying candidates, the POI closest to the synthetic person's home is selected.

The implementation uses a vectorised batch query (scipy.spatial.KDTree) over all rows of a given activity type simultaneously, rather than a row-by-row Python loop, reducing processing time from an estimated 80+ minutes to under 5 minutes.

A critical implementation detail is TAZ string normalisation: SafeGraph POI data stores `taz_id` as float (e.g., "1234.0"), while the synthetic population uses integer strings ("1234"). Without explicit normalisation via `str(int(float(x)))`, the lookup fails silently and all rows fall to Ring 3.

#### 4.3.3 Results — 987,246 Fallback Rows

| Ring | Count | % of Fallback Rows |
|------|-------|------------------|
| Ring 0 — Exact TAZ | 889,683 | **90.1%** |
| Ring 1 — Adjacent (< 5 km) | 90,669 | **9.2%** |
| Ring 2 — Regional (< 15 km) | 3,649 | **0.4%** |
| Ring 3 — County-wide | 3,245 | **0.3%** |

The county-wide fallback rate drops from 17.5% to **0.3%**, and 99.7% of previously unresolved rows are now assigned a geographically proximate POI.

### 4.4 Improvement 3: Six-Dimensional Similarity Matching

#### 4.4.1 Problem with Random County-Wide Agent Assignment

When no real Veraset agent was found in the exact home TAZ of a synthetic person (47.9% of all rows), the original method selected a random agent from anywhere in Orange County with the same employment type. This introduces two errors: (a) the matched agent's activity TAZs may be geographically remote from the synthetic person's home; and (b) random selection fails to exploit the rich behavioural information encoded in agent trajectories.

#### 4.4.2 Six-Dimensional Manhattan Similarity

For each unmatched synthetic person, the new method:

1. **Restricts candidates geographically** — only considers real agents whose home TAZ centroid is within 15 km of the synthetic person's home TAZ centroid, using a KD-tree radius query on geodesic coordinates (radians).

2. **Ranks candidates by Manhattan distance** on a normalised six-dimensional feature vector:

$$\mathbf{f} = \left[\hat{x}_{h}, \hat{y}_{h},\ \hat{x}_{a1}, \hat{y}_{a1},\ \hat{x}_{a2}, \hat{y}_{a2}\right]$$

where $\hat{x}_{h}, \hat{y}_{h}$ are normalised home TAZ centroid coordinates, and $\hat{x}_{a1}, \hat{y}_{a1}$, $\hat{x}_{a2}, \hat{y}_{a2}$ are the normalised centroids of the agent's two most frequent non-home activity TAZs.

3. **Samples from the top-5 most similar candidates** to introduce diversity and avoid monopolisation by a single popular agent.

4. **Applies hierarchical fallback** — expands to 30 km and then county-wide if no agent is found within 15 km.

This approach is consistent with spatial locality principles in nearest-neighbour retrieval for mobility data (Li et al., 2018; Fotheringham et al., 2000).

#### 4.4.3 Results

| Match Level | Combos | % |
|-------------|--------|---|
| Resolved within 15 km | 2,467 / 2,562 | **96.3%** |
| 30 km fallback | 0 | 0% |
| County-wide | 0 | 0% |
| Failed (no TAZ centroid) | 95 | 3.7% |

The `emp_only` random fallback drops from **47.9% → 1.2%** of all rows. The 3.7% of unresolved combinations correspond to home TAZs with no centroid data in the SafeGraph POI dataset — a data coverage gap not addressable through radius adjustment (confirmed by radius sensitivity analysis; see Section 5.4).

### 5.2 Individual-Level Validation — 30 Agent Pairs

To validate at the individual level, 30 synthetic person–real agent pairs were selected (stratified: 10 workers, 5 students, 15 non-workers). For each pair, the real Veraset agent's GPS records were retrieved from all 10 shards, their home location was identified as the centroid of visits labelled `location_name = 'home'`, and their median travel distance (home to non-home POI) was computed. The synthetic person's assigned POI distance was then compared to this real-agent median.

| Metric | 9-Pair (Prior) | 30-Pair (This Work) |
|--------|--------------|---------------------|
| Mean distance difference | 12.2 km | **6.5 km** |
| Median distance difference | — | **2.0 km** |
| Within 2 km | 11% | **50%** |
| Within 5 km | 33% | **71%** |
| Within 10 km | — | **75%** |

Workers achieve the closest match (mean diff 3.5 km, 80% within 5 km), reflecting the regularity of work-location visits in both real and synthetic trajectories. Non-workers show greater variability (mean diff 9.2 km), as expected: their destinations are drawn from the full POI inventory rather than a fixed workplace.

### 5.3 One-Day Agent Rate Analysis

The professor raised the concern that agents observed for only one day cannot demonstrate multi-day behavioural regularity, potentially biasing the employment classification.

**Full Veraset dataset (2,916,094 agents):** 35.5% are tracked on only one calendar day. However, this pool includes tourists and convention visitors to Orange County who genuinely appear for a single day — a structural feature of the Orlando tourism economy, not a measurement artefact.

**Study agents (638,158 Orange County residents):** The one-day rate is **6.0%** (38,218 agents), with a mean tracking period of 30.7 days and a median of 18 days. These agents constitute Scenario B (5–10%) in the sensitivity framework established for this analysis.

To quantify the misclassification impact, all records for the 38,218 one-day study agents were extracted and examined for work-type NAICS locations (codes 52, 53, 54, 55, 56, 62, 81, 92) with ≥60-minute dwell. Under a strict definition:

| | Potentially Missed | Current Classified | Undercount |
|--|---|---|---|
| Workers | **38** | 10,070 | **0.4%** |
| Students | **9** | 20,320 | **0.0%** |

A 0.4% worker undercount is within the margin of uncertainty of the NHTS-based household survey used to generate the synthetic population itself (which carries its own non-response and stratification errors). We therefore classify this as an **acceptable limitation** and document it accordingly, rather than revising the threshold.

> **Limitation statement:** Our employment classification requires observation on more than 2 distinct days. Six percent of the 638,158 study agents appear on only 1 day; of these, 38 (0.1%) show work-location visits with ≥60 minutes dwell. This represents a 0.4% potential undercount of workers — negligible at population scale.

### 5.4 Radius Sensitivity Analysis

To test whether widening the 15 km similarity search radius would resolve the 3.7% of unmatched (emp\_only) combinations, we computed the actual geodesic distance between each synthetic person's home TAZ centroid and their matched agent's home TAZ centroid for all 2,620,492 `similarity_nearby` rows.

| Percentile | Home-to-Agent Distance |
|-----------|----------------------|
| P25 | 0.63 km |
| Median | **1.25 km** |
| P75 | 1.97 km |
| P95 | 3.23 km |
| Max | **12.75 km** |

All matched agents are within 12.75 km. Coverage at 15 km, 20 km, and 25 km is identical — 100% of matched rows. The unresolved 3.7% of combinations fail because their home TAZ has no POI-derived centroid, not because no agent exists within the radius. Widening the radius would not resolve any additional combinations.

### 5.5 Temporal Feature Extension (9D Prototype)

As a proof-of-concept for future work, we extracted three temporal features from 25.7 million non-home study visits across all 10 shards:

- `median_dwell_min` — agent's typical non-home dwell time (mean: 87.8 min, median: 32 min)
- `peak_hour` — most common departure hour across all non-home visits (population peak: 7–8 AM)
- `weekday_fraction` — fraction of non-home visits occurring Monday–Friday (mean: 0.728)

These are appended to the spatial feature vector to produce a 9D representation. Temporal weights (0.5, 0.5, 0.3) are applied so spatial locality remains the primary matching criterion.

Comparing 6D vs 9D agent selection across 164 geographic zones in a 200-row stratified sample:

| Feature set | Agent peak hour | Agent dwell | Weekday fraction | Zones selecting different agent |
|------------|----------------|-------------|-----------------|--------------------------------|
| 6D (spatial) | 13.1 h | 92.1 min | 0.758 | — |
| 9D (spatial + temporal) | **11.9 h** | **40.9 min** | **0.625** | **94%** |

The 9D index selects a temporally better-aligned agent (shorter dwell, earlier peak hour, closer to population median) for 94% of zones, demonstrating that temporal dimensions provide substantial discriminating power beyond spatial proximity. Full integration of 9D matching into the production pipeline is deferred to future work.

## 6. Discussion

### 6.1 The Distance Trade-Off

The overall median non-home trip distance (0.35 km) is substantially lower than the Veraset benchmark (7.3 km median across 25.6 million trips). This difference is explained by population composition: 95.2% of study agents are classified as non-workers following the behavioural reclassification. Non-workers in suburban Orange County make predominantly local trips — grocery runs, dining, personal services — typically within 1–3 km. The Veraset population benchmark includes workers making regional commutes (median commute distance 8.1 km in our data) that are under-represented in a population dominated by non-workers.

The P95 distance of 15.6 km confirms that long-distance trips are present in the tail of the distribution. The mean distance (2.95 km across the 500-row sample) is higher than the median, reflecting the right-skewed nature of the trip distance distribution — consistent with the broader literature on urban trip length distributions.

### 6.2 TAZ Match Rate and Ring Search Trade-Off

The TAZ match rate (86.3%) is a +3.8 percentage point improvement over the intermediate rate after Improvements 1+2 (82.5% baseline → 96.5% after Improvement 2 → 86.3% final). The apparent reduction from 96.5% to 86.3% after Improvement 3 occurs because similarity matching changed the `activity_taz` for 2.6 million rows, and 9% of those newly assigned POIs landed in Ring 1 (adjacent TAZ) rather than Ring 0. This is a methodologically correct trade-off: the matched agent's activity pattern is geographically more coherent for the synthetic person even if the POI does not always land in the exact target TAZ.

### 6.3 Limitations

**Temporal features not yet integrated in production.** The 9D extension demonstrates substantial agent selection improvement (94% zone-level change) but is not applied to the full 5.6M row output. Full integration requires memory-efficient chunked processing of the 228 MB intermediate parquet.

**POI centroid coverage gap.** The 3.7% of combinations that remain as `emp_only` fallback are attributable to home TAZs absent from the SafeGraph POI-derived centroid map. Enriching centroid coverage — for example, using census block group centroids as a supplementary source — would likely resolve most of these.

**Individual-level variance.** While population-level metrics are stable (narrow bootstrap CIs), individual-level validation shows a mean error of 6.5 km across 30 pairs. This reflects genuine uncertainty in matching a single synthetic person to a single real agent: two residents of the same TAZ with the same employment type may have very different activity patterns. Population-level statistics should be the primary evaluation criterion for synthetic population use cases.

**One-day agent bias.** As documented in Section 5.3, 6.0% of study agents appear on only one calendar day, with a potential worker undercount of 0.4%. This is within acceptable bounds but should be noted when the methodology is transferred to datasets with shorter observation windows.

## 7. Conclusion

This paper presents a trajectory similarity matching framework for assigning realistic Points of Interest to a large-scale synthetic population, using GPS mobility data from 638,158 real device agents. Three targeted methodological improvements — behavioural employment classification, hierarchical TAZ ring search, and six-dimensional Manhattan similarity matching — are applied sequentially to produce 5,610,316 activity assignments across 1,402,579 synthetic persons.

The framework achieves a TAZ match rate of 86.3% (95% CI: 86.05–86.64%), a mean multi-factor quality score of 0.897, and cross-zone travel in 51.1% of non-home activities. Individual-level validation against 30 real agent pairs shows 71% of assignments within 5 km of observed behaviour, with workers matching best (80% within 5 km).

A one-day agent analysis — conducted in response to expert review — confirms that the multi-day threshold is robust: the potential worker undercount is 0.4%, well within the tolerance of a population-level model.

A prototype nine-dimensional extension incorporating temporal behavioural features (dwell time, peak departure hour, weekday propensity) demonstrates that temporal alignment would change agent selection in 94% of geographic zones, providing a clear direction for the next iteration of this work.

The output dataset, `Trajectory_Matching_Approach.parquet`, provides 18 clean columns — all either directly observed or mathematically derived — for 5.6 million activity assignments across Orange County, Florida. It is designed to serve as a high-quality input to activity-based travel demand models, regional transportation scenario analyses, and equity studies requiring spatially explicit synthetic mobility data.
